## Complete Prompt Analysis

### Prompt 1: Persuasive/Argumentative Essay

**Full Text:**
> "Do you think that manufacturers are responsible for the effects of the chemicals used in creating their products?"

#### Prompt Type Classification
- **Primary Type:** Persuasive/Argumentative Essay
- **Secondary Elements:** Expository (explaining reasoning)

#### Task Requirements

**Explicit Requirements:**
- Take a clear position (yes, no, or nuanced stance)
- Provide reasoned argument for the position
- Address the question of corporate responsibility

**Implicit Requirements:**
- Demonstrate understanding of environmental/health issues
- Show ability to construct logical arguments
- Consider multiple perspectives (even if arguing one side)
- Use evidence or reasoning to support claims

#### Skills Being Assessed

1. **Critical Thinking**
   - Ability to take and defend a position
   - Understanding of complex social/environmental issues
   - Evaluation of responsibility and accountability

2. **Argumentative Writing Skills**
   - Thesis statement formulation
   - Logical argumentation
   - Use of evidence and examples
   - Counterargument consideration
   - Persuasive techniques

3. **Content Knowledge**
   - Awareness of environmental/health concerns
   - Understanding of corporate responsibility concepts
   - Current events and real-world applications

4. **Organization**
   - Clear introduction with thesis
   - Body paragraphs with distinct arguments
   - Logical flow of ideas
   - Strong conclusion

#### Expected Response Structure

**Introduction (1 paragraph)**
- Hook/engaging opening
- Context about the issue
- Clear thesis statement taking a position
- Preview of main arguments

**Body (2-4 paragraphs)**
- Each paragraph focuses on one main argument
- Topic sentences for each paragraph
- Supporting evidence, examples, or reasoning
- Logical transitions between paragraphs
- May include counterargument and rebuttal

**Conclusion (1 paragraph)**
- Restate thesis (in different words)
- Summarize key arguments
- Final persuasive statement or call to action
- Leave reader with lasting impression

#### Assessment Criteria for Strong Response

**Content & Ideas (Selection and Development):**
- Clear, defensible position on manufacturer responsibility
- Well-developed arguments with depth
- Relevant examples (real or hypothetical)
- Consideration of complexity (not overly simplistic)
- Shows understanding of stakeholder perspectives

**Structure & Organization:**
- Logical progression of ideas
- Clear paragraphing with topic sentences
- Effective transitions
- Introduction that engages and states position
- Conclusion that reinforces argument

**Creativity & Sophistication:**
- Original insights or perspectives
- Nuanced understanding (acknowledging complexity)
- Sophisticated argumentation techniques
- Engaging writing style

**Vocabulary:**
- Precise, subject-specific terminology (e.g., "environmental impact," "corporate accountability," "sustainable practices")
- Varied vocabulary avoiding repetition
- Academic/formal tone appropriate to persuasive essay

**Technical Accuracy:**
- Correct grammar throughout
- Accurate spelling
- Proper sentence structure
- Appropriate punctuation

#### Example Strong Response Characteristics

**Thesis Examples:**
- Strong: "Manufacturers bear primary responsibility for the chemical effects of their products because they control production processes, have access to safer alternatives, and possess the resources to implement change."
- Weak: "I think manufacturers should be responsible because chemicals are bad."

**Argument Development:**
- Strong: Uses specific examples (e.g., reference to specific industry, historical cases, logical reasoning chains)
- Weak: General statements without support

**Vocabulary Sophistication:**
- Strong: "environmentally sustainable," "corporate accountability," "regulatory oversight," "mitigate harmful effects"
- Weak: "bad for environment," "they should be good," "make things better"

### Prompt 2: Creative/Open-Ended Response with Visual Stimulus

**Full Text:**
> "Use below picture as a stimulus for a piece of writing. You can write in any formation of text type which you think is appropriate."

**Visual Stimulus Description:**
A surreal/atmospheric photograph showing a man in formal attire (white shirt, dark vest/waistcoat, dark pants) walking on what appears to be a beach or shallow water. The scene has a moody, grey sky. An empty chair is positioned on the beach/water behind him. The image has a dreamlike, contemplative quality suggesting themes of solitude, journey, departure, or reflection.

#### Prompt Type Classification
- **Primary Type:** Open-ended creative writing with student choice of text type
- **Possible Text Types:**
  - Narrative (short story)
  - Descriptive piece
  - Reflective essay
  - Poem
  - Diary/journal entry
  - Letter
  - Monologue/soliloquy
  - Script/dialogue

#### Task Requirements

**Explicit Requirements:**
- Use the image as inspiration/stimulus
- Choose an appropriate text type/format
- Justify the choice through effective execution

**Implicit Requirements:**
- Demonstrate understanding of different text types
- Show strategic thinking in text type selection
- Create coherent connection between image and written response
- Demonstrate versatility as a writer
- Use "layout and arrangement" effectively for chosen text type

#### Skills Being Assessed

1. **Creative Interpretation**
   - Ability to interpret visual stimuli
   - Imaginative thinking
   - Creating meaning from abstract/symbolic imagery
   - Thematic development from visual cues

2. **Text Type Knowledge**
   - Understanding of different writing formats
   - Knowledge of conventions for various text types
   - Ability to select appropriate format for purpose

3. **Versatility**
   - Adaptability to different writing styles
   - Range of writing capabilities
   - Strategic decision-making

4. **Creative Writing Skills**
   - Imagery and descriptive language
   - Mood and atmosphere creation
   - Character development (if narrative)
   - Figurative language and literary devices
   - Voice and tone control

5. **Organization (Text Type Specific)**
   - Appropriate structure for chosen format
   - Effective use of paragraphing/stanza breaks
   - Layout choices that enhance meaning

#### Possible Interpretations and Text Type Matches

**Narrative Interpretation:**
- **Story angle:** Man leaving his old life behind (chair = past)
- **Story angle:** Magical realism - man walking on water
- **Story angle:** Last moments before a journey/transformation
- **Story angle:** Survivor of disaster finding emptiness
- **Text type suitability:** High - allows for plot, character, setting development

**Reflective/Philosophical Interpretation:**
- **Angle:** Meditation on solitude and purpose
- **Angle:** Contemplation of life's journey
- **Angle:** The weight of responsibility (formal attire in natural setting)
- **Text type suitability:** Reflective essay or personal essay format

**Poetic Interpretation:**
- **Angle:** Isolation and emptiness (empty chair, lone figure)
- **Angle:** Nature vs. civilization (formal wear, natural setting)
- **Angle:** Liminal spaces and transitions
- **Text type suitability:** High - poetry excellent for mood, symbolism, concise imagery

**Symbolic/Abstract Interpretation:**
- Empty chair = loss, absence, memory
- Walking on water = impossibility, transcendence, faith
- Formal attire = societal expectations, professionalism, constraint
- Grey sky = uncertainty, melancholy, transition

#### Expected Response Structure (Varies by Text Type)

**If Narrative (Short Story):**
- Clear beginning, middle, end (or purposeful in medias res)
- Character development
- Setting establishment
- Conflict or tension
- Resolution or meaningful ending
- Dialogue (if appropriate)
- Descriptive passages

**If Poem:**
- Deliberate structure (stanzas, line breaks)
- Imagery and figurative language
- Sound devices (if appropriate: rhythm, rhyme, alliteration)
- Cohesive theme or message
- Emotional resonance

**If Reflective Essay:**
- Introduction establishing the scene/reflection trigger
- Body exploring thoughts, feelings, realizations
- Personal insights and meaning-making
- Conclusion with resolution or lasting question

**If Descriptive Piece:**
- Vivid sensory details
- Organized spatial or temporal description
- Mood and atmosphere
- Figurative language
- Overall impression or significance

#### Assessment Criteria for Strong Response

**Selection & Appropriateness:**
- Text type choice makes sense for the interpretation
- Student demonstrates understanding of chosen format
- Format enhances rather than limits the expression

**Development & Ideas:**
- Clear connection to the visual stimulus
- Original interpretation (not cliched)
- Depth of exploration
- Coherent theme or message
- Effective use of details from image

**Creativity:**
- Unique or unexpected interpretation
- Imaginative elaboration
- Engaging and original voice
- Effective use of literary devices
- Risk-taking (ambitious vocabulary, complex ideas)

**Structure (Format-Specific):**
- Follows conventions of chosen text type
- Effective paragraphing/stanza breaks
- Logical organization
- Strong opening and closing
- Purposeful layout choices

**Sophisticated Vocabulary:**
- Precise descriptive language
- Varied word choice
- Subject-specific terminology (if appropriate)
- Figurative language and imagery
- Tone-appropriate vocabulary

**Technical Accuracy:**
- Correct grammar for chosen format
- Accurate spelling
- Appropriate punctuation (including format-specific punctuation)
- Sentence variety

#### Example Strong Response Characteristics

**Narrative Opening (Strong):**
"The chair sat empty, half-submerged in the shallows, its weathered frame a monument to someone I used to be. I couldn't bear to turn back. Each step forward into the grey horizon felt like walking on faith itself—or perhaps on the thin surface of my own dissolving certainty."

**Narrative Opening (Weak):**
"There was a man walking on the beach. He left a chair behind him. The sky was grey."

**Poetic Approach (Strong):**
```
The chair remembers—
wooden witness to conversations
now dissolved in salt and silence.

I walk forward, formally dressed
for an occasion no one named,
the water beneath my feet
both solid and surrendering.
```

**Poetic Approach (Weak):**
```
A man walks alone
The chair is empty
The sky is grey
He is sad
```

**Vocabulary Examples:**
- Strong: "desolate," "liminal," "weathered," "horizon," "dissolution," "formal restraint," "vastness," "contemplative"
- Weak: "sad," "alone," "walking," "grey," "empty"

## Strategic Guidance for Students

### Prompt 1 Strategy (Persuasive Essay)

**Planning Phase (2-3 minutes):**
1. Decide your position immediately
2. List 2-3 main arguments
3. Think of one example for each argument
4. Plan a counterargument if time allows

**Writing Phase (12-15 minutes):**
1. Write a clear thesis in introduction
2. One paragraph per main argument
3. Use transitions between paragraphs
4. Include specific examples
5. Write a strong concluding statement

**Quick Check (1-2 minutes):**
1. Scan for obvious spelling errors
2. Check that thesis is clear
3. Ensure conclusion restates position

### Prompt 2 Strategy (Creative Response)

**Planning Phase (2-3 minutes):**
1. Study the image carefully - note details, mood, symbolism
2. Decide on interpretation/theme
3. Choose text type that best fits your idea
4. Quick outline of structure

**Writing Phase (12-15 minutes):**
1. Create strong opening that establishes mood/situation
2. Develop ideas with rich detail
3. Use sophisticated vocabulary
4. Build to meaningful ending
5. Ensure connection to visual stimulus is clear

**Quick Check (1-2 minutes):**
1. Scan for spelling/grammar errors
2. Check that piece makes sense
3. Ensure appropriate structure for chosen text type

### Universal Tips

1. **Read instructions carefully** - note what's being asked
2. **Plan before writing** - even 2 minutes of planning helps
3. **Write neatly** - legibility matters
4. **Use paragraphs** - clear structure is essential
5. **Vary sentences** - mix short and long sentences
6. **Choose words carefully** - avoid repetition, use precise vocabulary
7. **Stay on topic** - every sentence should relate to prompt
8. **Manage time** - save 2 minutes for checking
9. **Show confidence** - write assertively, avoid "I think maybe"
10. **Proofread** - catch simple errors that hurt your score

## Practice Exercise Framework

### Skill-Building Exercises

#### Vocabulary Enhancement
- **Synonym replacement:** Take simple sentences and upgrade vocabulary
- **Word banks:** Create lists of sophisticated alternatives for common words
- **Context practice:** Use new vocabulary in various sentence contexts

#### Structure Practice
- **Paragraph frames:** Fill in structured paragraph templates
- **Transition drills:** Practice using connecting words between ideas
- **Outlining:** Convert prompts to outlines before writing

#### Text Type Mastery
- **Format studies:** Analyze examples of different text types
- **Quick writes:** 10-minute timed writes in specific formats
- **Conversion exercises:** Take same idea, write in different text types

#### Prompt Analysis
- **Identify task words:** Underline and define key words in prompts
- **Requirement checklists:** List what response must include
- **Choice mapping:** For open prompts, map possible approaches

### Full Practice Tests

**Structure:**
1. Present both prompts simultaneously (as in real test)
2. Set timer for 30-35 minutes total
3. Require students to manage time between prompts
4. Handwrite responses
5. Mark using rubric framework

**Debrief Focus:**
- Time management effectiveness
- Prompt interpretation accuracy
- Structural choices and effectiveness
- Vocabulary sophistication
- Grammar and mechanics
- Areas for improvement

## Appendix: Common Pitfalls and Solutions

### Common Student Mistakes

**Prompt 1 (Persuasive Essay):**
- ❌ Weak or missing thesis statement
  - ✅ Solution: Practice writing thesis statements separately

- ❌ Arguments without support
  - ✅ Solution: Teach claim-evidence-reasoning framework

- ❌ Informal tone ("I think," "you know")
  - ✅ Solution: Model academic language, practice formal register

- ❌ Lack of organization
  - ✅ Solution: Use paragraph templates and outlining

- ❌ Ignoring counterarguments
  - ✅ Solution: Teach counterargument-rebuttal structure

**Prompt 2 (Creative Response):**
- ❌ Choosing text type they can't execute well
  - ✅ Solution: Build confidence in 2-3 strong formats

- ❌ Weak connection to visual stimulus
  - ✅ Solution: Practice describing images first, then extending to writing

- ❌ Clichéd interpretations
  - ✅ Solution: Brainstorm multiple interpretations before choosing

- ❌ Starting too ambitiously and not finishing
  - ✅ Solution: Practice complete pieces in time limit

- ❌ Unclear or confusing structure
  - ✅ Solution: Review conventions of chosen text type before writing

**Both Prompts:**
- ❌ Poor time management
  - ✅ Solution: Timed practice with both prompts

- ❌ Repetitive vocabulary
  - ✅ Solution: Vocabulary building exercises

- ❌ Grammar/spelling errors
  - ✅ Solution: Focused mini-lessons on common errors

- ❌ Illegible handwriting
  - ✅ Solution: Handwriting practice, emphasis on neatness

- ❌ Not leaving time to proofread
  - ✅ Solution: Teach pacing, require proofreading in practice
